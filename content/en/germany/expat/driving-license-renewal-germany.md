## The First Reality After Moving to Germany

When you move to Germany, many things seem to remain the same. Your home, job, and daily routine slowly fall into place. However, there are topics you only realize too late. The driving license is one of them.

In the first few months after moving, no one will remind you that *"you won’t be able to drive a car in six months."* This information sits quietly in the background, and if you don’t notice it, the time simply runs out.

This post shares a first-hand experience of the driving license exchange process in Germany, the attempt via Bulgaria, and why this path didn’t work.

---

## Personal Background: From Turkey to Bulgaria, then to Germany

I was born and raised in Istanbul. Since we are Bulgarian immigrants on my father's side, I had a long-standing citizenship process. In August 2024, I obtained Bulgarian citizenship and became a dual citizen. In February 2025, I moved to Germany.

When I settled in Germany, my question was simple:
**How long is a driving license issued in Turkey valid in Germany?**

---

## The Legal Framework in Germany

The clock starts ticking the moment you register your address (**Anmeldung**). Since a license issued in Turkey is considered non-EU, it is **only valid for 6 months.**

After this period, there is no legal room for "it’ll be fine" or "they won’t check." Germany leaves no gray areas here. That's why I tried to understand the process early on.

### Which Countries' Licenses are Directly Valid in Germany?

Here is the general overview:

| License Country | Validity in Germany |
|---|---|
| EU member states | Valid indefinitely, no exchange required |
| Switzerland, Norway, Iceland | Directly valid |
| Turkey | **6 months** from Anmeldung |
| Ukraine, Serbia etc. | Depends on the country, check required |
| USA, Canada | Agreement depends on the state/province, usually 6 months |

## The Idea of the "Bulgarian Solution"

Being a Bulgarian citizen naturally opened another door. The thought: *"If I exchange my Turkish license for a Bulgarian one, I'll eventually have an EU license."* This is often the first reflex of many people in this situation.

There is significant information chaos online regarding this. Some say "it works," others say "I handled it through contacts," and others provide no details. This confusion is exactly why I am writing this post.

---

## Requirements for the Process in Bulgaria

I had an active residence permit in Bulgaria and a registered address in Sofia. This is important because without a registered address, you cannot even start this process in Bulgaria.

**Required documents:**

- Active address registration and residence in Bulgaria
- Original Turkish license + copy
- Passport photos
- Medical certificate suitable for license renewal
- Local language knowledge or interpreter support
- License fee *(payment by credit card is possible)*

---

## The Medical Certificate Process: Simpler than Expected

You don’t necessarily have to go to Sofia for the medical certificate. I did this at a small medical facility near Kardzhali. The doctor asked a few basic questions; there was no real physical exam. I paid about **30 leva** and received the certificate on the same day.

---

## Facing Reality at the KAT Centers in Sofia

The real truth begins when you enter the **KAT** (*Kontrol na Avtomobilniya Transport*) centers in Sofia. Signs, forms, and instructions are entirely in Bulgarian.

At the first KAT center, I was told my address wasn’t registered there and was directed to another center. At the second center, I took a number, waited, and finally stood before the officer. I tried to speak English, but the officer didn’t know English and didn’t want to process the application. I was told clearly: *"Come back with someone who speaks Bulgarian."*

---

## Documents, Language, and the Human Factor

On the next attempt, the copy of the license and passport photos were missing. I went out, got copies made, came back, and waited again. This time another officer came, and again the process stopped due to the language barrier.

At this point, you understand one thing clearly:
> **The system is theoretically official, but in practice, it is entirely dependent on the human factor.**

---

## Meeting the Interpreter: The Unwritten Rule of the System

In a nearby mall, thanks to a shop assistant who had gone through this process, I got the number of an interpreter who spoke Turkish and Bulgarian. With the interpreter, we returned to the center. This time everything moved fast. Forms were filled, questions were asked and answered.

**The plain truth is:** If you don’t speak Bulgarian, this process almost doesn’t work without an interpreter.

---

## The Waiting Period After Application

During the application, you are given a document. You must show this document when collecting the license — **do not lose it.**

After the application, you wait for about **30 days**. During this time, you keep your Turkish license and can legally continue to drive.

---

## Delivery of the License and a Critical Detail

Around the 25th day, I was notified that the license was ready. I went to Sofia for the day and completed the delivery process with interpreter support.

During delivery, my Turkish license was taken, and the Bulgarian license was given in its place. Everything seemed fine. **Until I looked at the back of the license.**

---

## The TR Code: The Summary of the Whole Process

On the back of the license, there was a code in the format **TR-XXXXX**. This code indicates that the country of original issue was Turkey.

This meant: **The document had changed, but the legal origin had not.**

---

## The Clear Answer from Germany

I went directly to the driving license office (**Führerscheinstelle**) in Germany and asked:
*"Can I use this license in Germany?"*

The answer was clear: **No.**

Even though the license was issued by Bulgaria, it was still treated as a Turkish license by Germany because its origin was non-EU.

---

## Why the Bulgarian Path Doesn't Work: Legal Explanation

When looking at the situation from the perspective of EU directives, it becomes clear. According to EU Driving License Directive **2006/126/EC**, a member state must recognize a license issued by another member state — but only if that license was issued according to that member state's own criteria.

When Bulgaria exchanges a Turkish license, the origin of the document is recorded. Germany sees this record and evaluates the license not as EU-based, but as Turkish-based.

So, this path looks attractive **in theory**, but it doesn't work **legally.**

---

## Facing the Mandatory Process in Germany

From here, the process is similar for everyone: driving school, theory test, practical lessons, and instructor approval. The fact that you have driven for years in Turkey doesn’t give you an automatic advantage here.

### What You Need to Know About the Theory Test

- The exam can be taken in German, English, or Turkish.
- Total of 30 questions, a maximum of 10 error points allowed.
- Official apps for studying: **DEGENER** or **Führerschein 2024**.
- Average study time: 4–8 weeks.

### About Practical Lessons

Driver training in Germany is very different from Turkey. The instructor will not send you to the exam until they see you as competent. How many lessons you take depends entirely on the instructor — this directly affects the cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Can I rent a car in Germany with a Turkish license?**
Within the first 6 months after Anmeldung, yes. Some rental companies may request additional documents.

**What happens if I exceed 6 months?**
Driving becomes legally prohibited. In the event of an accident, insurance may be considered invalid.

**Is Anmeldung required to start a driving school?**
No, Anmeldung is not mandatory to start. But it is required when taking the exam.

**Which city is cheaper to get a license?**
Small cities are usually cheaper. Munich and Hamburg are among the most expensive.

**Can the theory exam be taken in Turkish?**
Yes, the theory exam can be selected in Turkish.

## Final Result: There Are No Shortcuts

Looking back today, I can say one thing clearly: **Obtaining a license via Bulgaria is not a shortcut in Germany.**

Time was spent, money was spent, but the legal result was zero. If someone reading this is making a similar plan, the greatest gain would be to know this truth from the start.

The process in Germany is long and expensive, but it is the **only valid way.**

4. **Don’t research the Bulgaria path** — that’s what this post is for.
