# Working & Living in the Netherlands: What I Learned Before Moving

*I applied for jobs there, got a sponsorship offer, and researched everything. Here's what nobody tells you.*

---

The Netherlands was my first serious target when I decided to move abroad. I spent months researching, applied to companies, and got far enough in the process to receive a sponsorship offer. I didn't end up moving there — but I learned an enormous amount along the way.

This is what I wish I'd found in one place when I started.

---

## 🔍 Where to Start: Finding the Right Resources

Most expat job searches start on LinkedIn or generic job boards. But for the Netherlands specifically, there's a much better resource: **[Welcome to NL](https://www.welcome-to-nl.nl)** — a platform commissioned by the Dutch Ministry of Economic Affairs, built specifically to attract and guide international talent.

It covers everything: jobs by sector and region, immigration and work permits, housing, healthcare, childcare, and a step-by-step relocation app. If you're seriously considering the Netherlands, this should be your first stop.

The sectors with the most international recruitment are high-tech, semiconductors, AI, agriculture & food, life sciences, and logistics. Companies like ASML in Eindhoven are world leaders in their fields and actively hire internationally.

---

## 💼 Sponsorship & Work Permits

As a Turkish citizen (at the time without EU citizenship), I needed a company willing to sponsor a work permit. This is called the *kennismigrant* (knowledge migrant) route in Dutch.

The good news: **the Netherlands actively recruits international talent** and has a structured legal pathway for it. Companies familiar with the process can sponsor efficiently. The company I spoke with had done it before and was straightforward about it.

The requirement: your salary must meet a minimum threshold (updated annually) to qualify for the knowledge migrant permit. For most professional roles, this isn't a problem — Dutch tech and engineering salaries are competitive.

---

## 💰 The 30% Ruling: What It Is and What Changed

The *30% ruling* is a Dutch tax incentive for highly skilled migrants recruited from abroad. The idea: if you're relocating from more than 150km outside the Netherlands, you may qualify for a tax exemption on 30% of your gross salary for several years.

In practice, on a €100,000 gross salary, instead of being taxed on the full amount, you're taxed on €70,000. This significantly increases your net take-home pay.

**Important update:** the rules changed. The original scheme offered 30% for five years. The updated version runs as follows: 30% for the first two years, 20% for years three and four, and 10% in the fifth year. Still valuable — but less generous than it was.

---

## 🏥 Healthcare: The Hidden Cost

This was one of the biggest surprises in my research. Unlike Germany's employer-matched statutory insurance, **the Netherlands requires individuals to purchase their own basic health insurance** (*zorgverzekering*).

The cost: roughly €120–130 per person per month for basic coverage. For a family of four, that's approximately €500 per month coming directly out of your net salary — on top of everything else.

The coverage at the basic level is also narrower than Germany's GKV. Dental, physiotherapy, and certain specialist treatments often require supplementary insurance (*aanvullende verzekering*) at additional cost.

When you factor this into your net salary calculation, the numbers look meaningfully different than they appear on the contract.

---

## 🏠 Housing: The Hardest Part

The Netherlands has a well-documented housing crisis, particularly in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and Eindhoven. Demand dramatically outpaces supply, and rental prices reflect this.

When I was researching, minimum realistic rents in the Amsterdam area started around **€1,750 per month** for a basic apartment. For a family, you're looking at significantly more.

There is one interesting counterbalance: the mortgage system. After just **one year of employment** in the Netherlands, you can apply for a mortgage (*hypotheek*). Some people I researched had found that a monthly mortgage payment on a property — around €900–1,000 — was actually lower than the equivalent rent for a similar space.

This is unusual in European terms. Most countries require several years of residency and income history before mortgage eligibility. The Dutch one-year rule is a genuine advantage for people planning to stay long-term.

---

## 🧒 Children & Education

Child development philosophy in the Netherlands is notably progressive. In the early primary years, **homework is minimal or absent** — the focus is on play, social development, and building confidence rather than academic performance.

The results speak for themselves: Dutch children consistently rank among the happiest in the world, and the Netherlands regularly appears in the top ten of global wellbeing indices.

For working families, there's a practical system worth knowing: after school (which often ends around 2–3pm), many areas have *BSO* (*buitenschoolse opvang* — out-of-school care) centres. These collect children from school and keep them until parents finish work. It's an additional cost, but it's structured and widely used.

---

## 🌍 The Language Question

One of the Netherlands' biggest advantages for international arrivals: **English is spoken at a genuinely high level** by the vast majority of the population. You can navigate daily life, work in many companies, and integrate socially without learning Dutch immediately.

This is a meaningful practical difference from Germany, where German is essential for most official and social contexts from day one.

Dutch is not impossible to learn — it has structural similarities to English and German. But it's not a prerequisite for getting started.

| Topic | Netherlands |

| Healthcare cost | Individual expense (~€500/month family) |

| Mortgage access | Fast (1 year) — unusual advantage |

| Work-life balance | Among the best in Europe |

---

## 💬 My Conclusion

The Netherlands is a genuinely attractive destination — progressive, international, English-friendly, with strong worker protections and a remarkable child development philosophy. The 30% ruling makes the financial case even stronger for high earners.

The housing situation is the biggest practical obstacle. Without a clear plan for accommodation before you arrive, the process becomes very stressful very quickly.

I came close to making this move. What ultimately pointed me toward Germany was a combination of factors — including the healthcare structure and the housing uncertainty. But for the right person in the right sector, the Netherlands remains one of Europe's strongest options.
