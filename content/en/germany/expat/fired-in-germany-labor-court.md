# Being Fired in Germany: My Labor Court Experience

*Nobody tells you how this works. I lived through it — here's every detail.*

---

Getting fired in Germany works very differently from Turkey — or most other countries. There's a strict legal process, formal notification requirements, and rights you will lose if you don't act on them. This is my personal account of what happened, step by step.

---

## 📬 Written Notice is Mandatory — and the Delivery Date is Everything

In Germany, a termination notice must be delivered **in writing, by post**. This seems like a small detail. It determines everything.

Here's how it works: the company wrote the termination letter on January 15th. It arrived at your home on January 20th. **Your official notice date is January 20th** — not the date the company wrote it.

Your notice period — whatever your contract says, one month, two months, three — begins from that delivery date. If your contract says one month, your employment ends on February 20th.

**In my case:** the company sent the termination notice **by email**. This was a critical mistake on their part — and it ended up working in my favour.

---

## ⚠️ Is Email Termination Valid in Germany?

No. A valid employment termination in Germany requires a **wet signature on a physical document, delivered by post**.

When I went to the labor court (*Arbeitsgericht*) with only a printed email, they refused to accept my application. The formal requirements had not been met.

The company then sent a second notice — but this time they had simply printed the email and signed it. Still no proper wet-ink signature on an original document. On my second visit, the court said:

*"There's no properly delivered notice, but something did arrive by post. You have grounds to contest this."*

And that's how the process began.

---

## 🏛️ The Labor Court Process — Step by Step

### 1. The Jobcenter Sends You to Court

When I went to the Jobcenter to apply for unemployment benefits, they told me clearly: if I didn't take the case to labor court, **I would not be eligible for social support**. The court process wasn't optional — the Jobcenter required it.

### 2. The Document Problem at First Application

At my first court visit, they couldn't accept my application due to missing documents. But they gave me a **written confirmation that my application had been refused and why**. I took this to the Jobcenter — at least I could prove I had tried and explain why the process hadn't moved forward. This document was crucial.

### 3. Your Right to an Interpreter

Since my German wasn't strong enough, I requested an interpreter. **The court provided one at no cost to me.** This is your right — use it without hesitation.

### 4. The First Hearing and Compensation

The judge reviewed the situation and said: *"You were not terminated properly, but you cannot claim wages for the period you didn't work."*

Instead, compensation (*Abfindung*) was put on the table. The company made an offer. I tried to negotiate higher. The judge doesn't intervene in this discussion — it's between you and the employer.

**Important:** at the first hearing, you can represent yourself without a lawyer. From the second hearing onwards, legal representation becomes necessary.

---

## 💼 Legal Insurance — My Biggest Regret

The thing I regret most from this entire experience: **not having legal insurance** (*Rechtsschutzversicherung*).

In Germany, you can take out legal insurance for approximately **€260 per year**. This covers:

- Legal consultation fees
- Lawyer costs in labor court proceedings
- Representation from the second hearing onwards

I was unemployed when the situation arose and hadn't taken out this insurance. The consequences:

- I paid **€220 for a single consultation** with a lawyer — we spoke for less than an hour
- If the case had gone to a second hearing, I would have faced **€2,000–3,000 in lawyer fees**
- With legal insurance, all of this would have been covered

**My advice:** take out legal insurance as soon as you start working in Germany. At €260 per year, it can save you thousands in a single legal situation.

---

## 📋 The Compensation Agreement — What to Watch Out For

The judge said: *"If you don't agree today, the next hearing is in 3–4 months. You won't receive any payment during that time."*

We didn't want to wait. We calculated and agreed. But there's one critical lesson from this:

**Make sure the payment deadline is written into the court record.**

We didn't do this. The company delayed the payment — they said it would be paid by the 25th of the following month, then didn't pay. I had to threaten them — "if you don't pay, I'll reopen the case" — before they finally transferred the money.

---

## 📄 Your Right to a Reference Letter

I also requested something else from the court: a **good reference letter** (*Arbeitszeugnis*).

Reference letters matter enormously in Germany. I specified that it should contain "good" or "very good" language. The company's first version used neutral phrasing — they hadn't honoured the agreement. I had to push again before they sent a proper one.

**Write the quality standard for the reference letter into the court record.** "Good or very good" — explicitly stated.

---

## 💰 Jobcenter and Compensation — The Surprise Deduction

After everything was settled, I got an unpleasant surprise: the Jobcenter counted my compensation as **income** and significantly reduced my social support for the following month.

At the court hearing, I had asked the judge twice: *"If I accept this compensation, will I have to repay the Jobcenter? Will there be any deduction?"*

The judge said no — both times. My interpreter even asked again to make sure. Still no.

But the Jobcenter calculated it differently. I don't have a clean solution to this — but if you're in a similar situation: **ask the Jobcenter directly how they will treat your compensation before you agree to an amount.**

---

## 📌 Summary: 7 Things You Need to Know

1. **Termination must be by post with a wet signature.** Email is not valid.
2. **The notice date is when it arrives at your home.** Not when the company wrote it.
3. **The Jobcenter may require you to go to labor court.** It can be a condition of receiving benefits.
4. **You have the right to a free court interpreter.** Use it.
5. **Take out legal insurance.** ~€260/year can save you thousands.
6. **Write the payment deadline into the court record.** Without it, companies delay.
7. **Specify reference letter quality in the record.** "Good or very good" — in writing.
