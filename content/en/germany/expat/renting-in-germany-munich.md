## Renting in Germany: Why Is It So Hard?

Before moving to Germany, it's natural to think "we'll find something to rent." But the reality is: especially in big cities like Munich, Hamburg and Berlin, finding a rental feels like a full-time job. Days of scrolling listings, dozens of applications, very few responses.

For foreigners, this process becomes even harder. If you don't speak German, if you have no Schufa score, or if your employment contract just started — everything becomes more difficult.

In this article I share the Munich rental search process, which documents are required, which platforms actually work and what doesn't — all based on direct experience.

---

## Germany's Rental Market: The Big Picture

Tenant rights in Germany are very strong. While this sounds good for tenants, it makes landlords extremely selective about tenant choice. The result: the application process is as formal as a job application.

**Munich 2025 average rents:**

| Apartment type | Average rent |
|---|---|
| Studio | 1,200–1,800 EUR |
| 1 bedroom | 1,500–2,200 EUR |
| 2 bedrooms | 2,000–3,000 EUR |
| 3 bedrooms | 2,500–4,000 EUR |

Prices increase closer to the city center and with better public transport connections. Nebenkosten (additional costs) are generally not included in rent — electricity, water and heating are separate.

## What Documents Are Required?

Standard document package for a rental application in Germany:

**1. Schufa Auskunft**
Germany's credit reference document. Think of it as a financial background check. Those moving to Germany for the first time won't have a Schufa score — this is a major disadvantage. Can be requested online via Schufa.de, ~30 EUR.

**2. Einkommensnachweise (Income Proof)**
Last 3 months' payslips. Your net salary is expected to be 3 times the rent — meaning for 1,500 EUR rent, at least 4,500 EUR net income.

**3. Mietschuldenfreiheitsbescheinigung**
A "no rental debt" certificate from your previous landlord. If moving from Turkey, a Turkish-language letter plus translation may work. If renting for the first time, you won't have this — another disadvantage.

**4. Personalausweis / Reisepass Kopie**
ID or passport photocopy.

**5. Arbeitsvertrag (Employment Contract)**
Whether it's fixed-term or permanent matters enormously. A permanent contract (unbefristete Stelle) is much stronger.

**6. Selbstauskunft**
A personal information form provided by the landlord. Questions about how many people will live there, pets, smoking etc.

## WG: The Shared Apartment Option

For those newly arrived in Germany, WG (Wohngemeinschaft — shared apartment) is a much more accessible starting point.

**WG advantages:**
- Schufa score usually not required
- Less paperwork
- Much more affordable (room in Munich: 600–1,000 EUR)
- Gives you time to get to know the city

**WG disadvantages:**
- Not suitable for families
- Common areas are shared
- Not a long-term solution

Wg-gesucht.de is the best platform for this. When creating your profile, present yourself well — choosing a flatmate is also a process in Germany.

## How Does the Application Process Work?

1. **See the listing → apply immediately.** Popular listings in Munich close within hours.
2. **Write a personalized cover letter** — sending just documents isn't enough. Briefly introduce yourself, your work, and why you want this apartment.
3. **Get a Besichtigungstermin (viewing appointment)** — viewings are often held with multiple candidates simultaneously.
4. **Have your document package ready** — being able to hand it over at the viewing is an advantage.
5. **Wait for feedback** — sometimes there's silence for weeks; this is normal.

## Extra Challenges for Foreigners

**No Schufa:** Since you have no history in Germany, your Schufa score is zero. Counter-measures:
- Offer a higher deposit (6 months instead of 3)
- Provide a Bürge (guarantor) — must be someone resident in Germany
- Get a strong reference letter from your employer

**Language barrier:** The vast majority of listings and contracts are in German. Use the DeepL translation tool, and always get important documents translated before signing.

**Receiving "no" answers:** Applying to dozens of places and getting no responses is very common. This is not personal — landlords often prefer local Germans with established references.

---

## Once You Sign a Lease

**Kaution (Deposit):** Maximum 3 months' rent. Must be deposited in a tenant account — the landlord cannot use this money. Returned when you move out if there's no damage.

**Mieterverein membership:** Consider joining a tenants' association — about 10 EUR per month, provides legal support in any disputes.

**Übergabeprotokoll:** Key handover record. Document the condition of the apartment at handover — photograph every scratch and mark. You'll need this when moving out.

## My Experience

I started searching 2 months before moving to Munich. I got an ImmobilienScout24 Premium membership and applied to 3-4 listings per day. Zero responses in the first month. In the second month I got 3 viewings, one of which came back positive.

Since I had no Schufa score, I offered extra deposit. My employer provided a reference letter. The process took 2.5 months.

**Most critical lesson:** Start searching before you move to Germany. Temporary accommodation (Airbnb, hotel) after arrival is extremely expensive.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can you find an apartment without Schufa?**
Difficult but not impossible. A higher deposit offer, strong employer reference and a personal cover letter make a difference.

**Is Anmeldung required for a rental contract?**
No — you sign the rental contract first, then do Anmeldung. But you need the rental contract to do Anmeldung.

**How much are Nebenkosten?**
Usually 20-30% of rent. On 1,500 EUR rent, expect 300-450 EUR Nebenkosten.

**Can you find a rental with pets?**
Much harder. Landlord approval is required and many refuse.

**Are there cheaper alternatives outside Munich?**
Yes — surrounding cities like Augsburg, Landsberg am Lech, Freising are 30-40% cheaper. S-Bahn connections to Munich are good.
