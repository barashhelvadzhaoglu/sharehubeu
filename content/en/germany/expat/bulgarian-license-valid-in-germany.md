## "If I Get a Bulgarian License, Will Germany Accept It?" — The Clear Answer

For Turkish people who have moved to Germany and hold Bulgarian citizenship, this question is inevitable. The logic goes like this:

> *"I'm a Bulgarian citizen. If I convert my Turkish license to a Bulgarian one, I'll have an EU license. Germany will accept it."*

This logic is **theoretically attractive, practically wrong.** I tried this route. I spent time, I spent money, I made several trips to Sofia. The result: Germany does not recognize this license.

In this article, I'm telling you that process step by step, exactly as it happened.

---

## Personal Background

I was born and raised in Istanbul. Since my father's side are Bulgarian immigrants, I had a long-running citizenship process. **In August 2024, I obtained Bulgarian citizenship** and became a dual citizen. In February 2025, I moved to Germany.

After settling in Germany, the first alternative that came to mind: *"I'll convert my Turkish license to a Bulgarian one, problem solved."*

There's serious information chaos online about this topic. Some say "it works," others say "I sorted it through contacts," and others say "nobody asked me anything" without giving any details. The reason I'm writing this article is precisely that confusion.

---

## What's Required to Transfer a License in Bulgaria?

To do this in Bulgaria, you first need an **active residence permit and registered address.** Without an address registration, you can't even start the process.

**Required documents:**
- Active address registration and residence in Bulgaria
- Original Turkish license + photocopy
- Passport photos
- Medical certificate suitable for license renewal
- Knowledge of Bulgarian or interpreter support
- License fee (credit card accepted)

### Medical Certificate: Simpler Than Expected

You don't necessarily need to go to Sofia for the medical certificate. I did this at a small medical facility near Kardzhali. The doctor asked a few basic questions — there was no real examination. I paid about **30 leva** and received the certificate the same day.

---

## Sofia KAT Centers: Where Reality Begins

**KAT** *(Kontrol na Avtomobilniya Transport)* — the institution that handles traffic and license matters in Bulgaria. There are multiple centers in Sofia.

When I entered the first center, everything was in Bulgarian: signs, forms, instructions. The number of English-speaking staff was extremely low.

### What Happened on the First Attempt?

At the first center I was told my address wasn't registered there and was directed to another center. At the second center I took a number, waited, and stood before the officer. I tried to speak English — the officer didn't know English and didn't want to process the application. It was clearly stated: *"Come back with someone who speaks Bulgarian."*

### What Happened on the Second Attempt?

On the next attempt, the photocopy and passport photos were missing. I went out, got them, came back, took another number. This time a different officer came, and again the process stopped due to the language barrier.

At this point you understand one thing clearly:
> **The system is officially theoretical, but in practice entirely dependent on the human factor.**

### Without an Interpreter, It Simply Doesn't Work

In a nearby mall, through a shop assistant who had gone through this process, I got the number of an interpreter who spoke Turkish and Bulgarian. With the interpreter, we returned to the center. This time everything moved fast. Forms were filled, questions asked and answered.

**The plain truth:** If you don't speak Bulgarian, this process almost doesn't work without an interpreter.

---

## After Application: 30-Day Wait

During the application, you're given a document. This is needed when collecting the license — **don't lose it.**

The waiting period is approximately **30 days.** During this time you keep your Turkish license and can legally continue driving.

---

## I Collected the License — And Everything Changed

Around day 25, I received notification that the license was ready. I made a day trip to Sofia and completed the collection with interpreter support.

During collection, my Turkish license was taken and the Bulgarian license was given in its place. Everything seemed fine.

**Until I looked at the back of the license.**

---

## The TR Code: The Summary of the Entire Process

On the back of the license was a code in the format **TR-XXXXX**.

This code indicates that **the country of original issue was Turkey.** The document had changed, but the legal origin had not.

---

## The Clear Answer From Germany

I went directly to the driving license office **(Führerscheinstelle)** in Germany and asked:

*"Can I use this Bulgarian license in Germany?"*

The answer was clear and definitive: **No.**

Even though the license was issued by Bulgaria, because its origin was non-EU, Germany still treated it as a Turkish license.

---

## Why Doesn't It Work? The Legal Explanation

**EU Driving License Directive 2006/126/EC** clearly states: A member state must recognize a license issued by another member state — **but only if that license was issued according to that member state's own criteria.**

When Bulgaria converts a Turkish license, it records the origin of the document. Germany sees this record and evaluates the license not as EU-based, but as Turkish-based.

**Conclusion:** This route looks attractive in theory, but legally it doesn't work.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can't I transfer my Turkish license if I'm a Bulgarian citizen?**
You can transfer it — but the resulting Bulgarian license is evaluated in Germany the same as a Turkish license. Because the EU directive records the country of origin.

**Are there people who've successfully used this route?**
Online there are people saying "I sorted it" but I couldn't find evidence of any of them getting official confirmation from a Führerscheinstelle. The official answer is clear: no.

**Doesn't dual citizenship provide an advantage here?**
For citizenship matters, yes. For driving license matters, no. The license directive looks at the **country where the license was first issued**, not citizenship.

**What if I get a fresh license from scratch in Bulgaria?**
In theory, yes — but this requires genuinely residing in Bulgaria, passing the theory test in Bulgarian, and completing practical training there. Very difficult in practice.

**Is there a lawyer who knows about this?**
Specialized lawyers exist, but those I consulted reached the same conclusion: the EU directive is clear, there's no legal basis for appeal.
