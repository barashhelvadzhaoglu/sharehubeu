# Social Life & Making Friends: Turkey vs Germany

*"Germans are cold." Is it true? It's more complicated than that.*

---

## 🇹🇷 Turkey: Warm, Fast, and Deep

Turkish social culture is famously warm. Strangers become friends quickly. Hospitality is almost a competitive sport — guests must be fed, tea must be offered, no one leaves without feeling welcomed.

Friendships in Turkey tend to form fast and run deep. Social circles overlap: family, neighbours, school friends, work colleagues — they all mix. The boundaries between acquaintance and close friend collapse quickly.

The downside is that privacy can be harder to protect. Everyone has an opinion about your life choices. The collective nature of Turkish social culture is a warmth and a pressure simultaneously.

---

## 🇩🇪 Germany: Slow to Open, Loyal When They Do

Germans have a reputation for being cold or unfriendly. In my experience, this is a misreading. They're not cold — they're **reserved**. There's a difference.

Germans tend to keep their private and public spheres very separate. Small talk with strangers is less common. Someone won't smile at you on the street for no reason — not because they dislike you, but because that would feel performative to them.

But when a German decides you are their friend, that friendship is serious. They show up. They're reliable. They don't cancel plans. They remember what matters to you.

The challenge for expats is getting past the initial reserve. It takes longer. Joining clubs (*Vereine*), sports teams, language groups, or neighbourhood events are the most reliable ways in. Germans organize their social lives around structured activities more than spontaneous gatherings.

---

## ⚖️ The Real Comparison

| | Turkey | Germany |
|---|---|---|
| Initial warmth | Very high | Low to moderate |
| Speed of friendship | Fast | Slow |
| Depth of friendship | Deep | Deep (once established) |
| Hospitality culture | Very strong | Modest |
| Privacy norms | Low | High |
| Small talk culture | Natural | Less common |
| Expat community | Growing | Large and active |

---

## 💬 My Take

The loneliness of the first months in Germany is real — and nobody warns you about it enough. You move from a culture where social connection is effortless to one where you have to work for it. That's hard.

But I've come to deeply respect German friendship. When someone here makes time for you, it means something. It wasn't given out of politeness or habit — it was chosen.

The expat community in Munich also helped enormously. Finding other people navigating the same adjustment made everything easier.
