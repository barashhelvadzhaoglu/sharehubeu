# Trust as a Way of Life: What Germany Taught Me About Believing in People

*Living in Munich as an expat from Turkey — small details that changed how I see society*

---

Germany surprised me. Not with its punctuality, not with its efficiency — but with something quieter and harder to name: **trust**. The kind of trust that doesn't ask for proof. The kind that assumes you'll do the right thing, even when no one is watching.

Here are the moments that made me feel it.

---

## 🚌 Public Transport: No Turnstiles, No Guards

When I first took the bus in Munich, I waited for the gate. There was no gate. No ticket inspector at the door, no obligation to board from the front. You buy your ticket — or you don't — and you ride.

This isn't a loophole. It's a **deliberate design choice**. The system trusts that most people are honest, and that the cost of treating everyone like a potential cheater is greater than the cost of occasional free riders. Coming from a country where every bus has a driver watching every passenger, this felt almost radical.

---

## 📰 Unmanned Newspaper Stands

At street corners across Germany, you'll find wooden racks filled with newspapers. No vendor. No camera. No lock.

You take what you want, leave the money, and walk away. The math works — not because Germans are saints, but because the **social contract is taken seriously**. Defecting from it carries its own weight.

---

## 🥕 Farm Stalls Without Farmers

Drive through the countryside around Munich and you'll spot them: small wooden tables by the road, loaded with tomatoes, carrots, or potatoes — portioned, priced, unattended. A handwritten sign. A tin can for coins.

No one watching. No security. Just produce and a price.

These **Hofläden** (farm stalls) are quietly one of the most powerful symbols of civic trust I've encountered. They only work if people let them work — and somehow, they do.

---

## 🏠 The Honest Utility Bill

In Germany, rent often comes in two forms: *Kaltmiete* (cold rent — the base) and *Warmmiete* (warm rent — including heating and utilities). At the end of the year, landlords and tenants sit down together and calculate actual usage.

Who used more hot water? Who kept the heating on all winter? The adjustment is made honestly, based on real numbers — sometimes you owe more, sometimes you get money back.

No one is surveilled. The **assumption is honesty**, not suspicion.

---

## 🏖️ Toys Left Outside All Summer

Almost every apartment building in Munich has a small playground — a slide, a swing, a sandbox. And in that sandbox, all summer long, you'll find children's toys: buckets, shovels, toy trucks, sometimes even bikes left leaning against the fence overnight.

No one takes them.

I watched this for weeks before I believed it. These toys belong to the children of the building, but in a sense they belong to all the children. **No fence needed. No lock. Just shared space, shared respect.**

---

## 🧤 Lost and Found — On the Street

Drop a glove in Munich. Come back an hour later. It will likely still be there — or hanging on a nearby railing, placed carefully by a stranger so you can spot it when you retrace your steps.

I've seen this with gloves, scarves, keys, headphones. Once, with a phone.

It's such a small act, but it speaks to something large: the instinct here isn't *"this is unclaimed, I could take it"* — it's *"this belongs to someone, I'll make it easier for them to find."*

---

## 💬 What This Means

I'm not saying Germany is perfect. I'm not saying this is unique to Germany. And I know that trust at this scale is partly a product of systems, history, and demographics that are complicated.

But living here — in Munich, day to day — **I feel it**. A low-level sense of safety that isn't just about crime statistics. It's about walking through a city that assumes the best of you, and somehow making you want to live up to it.

That's a rare thing. And it's worth noticing.
