# Why I Left Turkey: The Decision That Changed Everything

*A personal account of weighing family, freedom, and the future — from someone who actually made the move*

---

Moving to another country is never just a practical decision. It's emotional, messy, and deeply personal. I've been asked many times: *"Why did you leave?"* This is my honest answer.

---

## 🧳 I'd Done It Before

After university, I spent about a year living in England. I got a taste of life abroad — the independence, the new perspectives, the quiet satisfaction of figuring out a foreign city.

But I came back. Back then, **family pulled harder than anything else**. My parents were in Turkey. My roots were there. The idea of building a life far from them felt like a loss I wasn't ready for.

So I returned. And for a while, that felt right.

---

## 👨‍👩‍👧 Then Everything Changed

Time passed. I got married. We had a child. And suddenly the question wasn't *"where do I want to live?"* but *"where do I want my child to grow up?"*

Those are very different questions.

When I started looking at the world through my child's eyes — the school system, the streets, the opportunities, the safety — my calculus shifted completely. **The future stopped being abstract.** It had a face.

---

## 👴 The Hardest Part: Leaving Family Behind

I won't pretend this was easy. My parents, my extended family — they're still in Turkey. They wanted us close. And a part of me will always feel the weight of that distance.

But I've come to believe that **building a future for your children sometimes means making sacrifices in the present**. Choosing the life you want for them, even when it costs you something real.

This doesn't make the distance hurt less. It just makes it feel necessary.

---

## 🧒 The Children Argument

One of the things that pushed me most decisively was reading about child development in Northern Europe — particularly the Netherlands, but Germany too.

What struck me most: **in the early school years, children aren't given homework**. The philosophy is that childhood itself is the education. Play, exploration, boredom even — these aren't obstacles to development, they *are* development.

The result? The Netherlands consistently ranks among the happiest countries in the world, and their children among the happiest children. That's not a coincidence.

I also noticed something on the streets of Munich that I couldn't unsee: **7 and 8-year-olds cycling to school alone**. No parent escort, no anxiety — just a child on a bike, navigating the city with confidence. That kind of independence, given early, shapes a person for life.

---

## ⚖️ What Tipped the Scale

It wasn't one thing. It was the accumulation:

- Watching the Turkish lira lose value year after year, and feeling the floor shift beneath my savings
- Worrying about road safety, construction standards, accountability — or the lack of it
- Seeing the education system struggle with trust and credibility
- Wanting my child to grow up in a place where **the rules apply to everyone equally**

None of these made Turkey unlivable. But together, they made the question impossible to ignore.

---

## 🎯 My Advice to Anyone Considering This

**Define your own criteria.** Mine won't be yours.

For some people, family proximity will always outweigh everything else — and that's a completely valid choice. For others, economic stability or educational opportunity comes first. There's no universal right answer.

What matters is that you ask the question honestly, without letting fear or inertia answer it for you.

I asked. And my answer led me to Munich.
