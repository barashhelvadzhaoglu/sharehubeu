## Regensburg: Germany's Best-Preserved Medieval City

Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2006, Regensburg has preserved its medieval texture to a remarkable degree — one of Europe's rarest examples. About 1,500 historic buildings, most from the Middle Ages, all still in use today.

Tower houses built by Italian merchants give the city a skyline unlike any other German city.

---

## Getting There

**Train:** Regional Express from Munich Hauptbahnhof, ~1.5–2 hours. Deutschlandticket valid.

---

## Places to Visit

### 1. Steinerne Brücke (Stone Bridge)
Regensburg's symbol. Dating from the 12th century over the Danube — once Europe's longest stone bridge. Still open to pedestrians. From the bridge, the cathedral's twin towers and city skyline are incredible.

**Tip:** Early morning or sunset — the bridge looks different at both times.

### 2. Dom St. Peter (Cathedral)
Bavaria's most important Gothic cathedral. Started in the 13th century, completed in the 19th. Massive interior — stained glass, sculptural details and acoustics are impressive. Free entry.

**Regensburg Domspatzen:** The cathedral's associated boys' choir, one of the world's oldest. Hearing them at Sunday services is a special experience.

### 3. Porta Praetoria
Roman-era northern gate. From 179 AD. Found in a shop's backyard — visible from inside. A direct trace of Rome.

### 4. Italian Tower Houses (Geschlechtertürme)
In the Middle Ages, wealthy merchant families built towers as symbols of power and prestige. About 20 of these towers still stand in Regensburg. **Baumburger Turm** and **Goldener Turm** are must-sees.

These tower houses create a skyline similar to Italian medieval cities (like San Gimignano) — nothing like it in Germany.

### 5. Altstadt Walk
Street by street, walking is an experience in itself. Every corner holds a historic building, every building a centuries-old story. No tourist crowds — real city life going on.

### 6. Danube Riverside
Both banks of the Steinerne Brücke along the riverside. In summer locals picnic and swim here. Lively atmosphere.

**Historische Wurstküche:** Right beside the Stone Bridge, a historic sausage kitchen operating in the same spot since the 12th century. Crowded but at least get a sausage and eat it by the river.

### 7. Schloss Thurn und Taxis
Giant palace open as museum inside the city. Built over a Carolingian-era monastery. Interior tour ~15 EUR but impressive.

---

## Food

**Historische Wurstküche** — mandatory stop. Small, historic, by the river. Sausage + mustard + Sauerkraut. Long queue possible but worth it.

Regensburg is more affordable than Munich — many options around the Altstadt.

---

## Practical Information

**How long?** 5–6 hours to comfortably see the city. Add Schloss Thurn und Taxis for a full day.

**Stroller:** Altstadt is largely cobblestone — difficult for strollers. Danube riverside and wider streets are good.

**Best season:** Spring and autumn are best. Summer has crowds but not as intense as Munich.

---

## FAQ

**How to get to Regensburg from Munich?** Regional Express, ~1.5 hours. Deutschlandticket valid.

**Can Regensburg be seen in one day?** Yes, a full day is plenty — leave early morning, return in the evening.

**Is Regensburg UNESCO?** Yes, UNESCO World Heritage since 2006 — the Altstadt and Stadtamhof.

**Does Historische Wurstküche need a reservation?** No. Queue up and go. Eaten outside by the river.
